Raft and Mat Foundation Design for Central Florida Karst Terrain

Together, we solve the challenges of tomorrow.

DISCOVER →

Orlando’s rapid expansion from a quiet citrus hub into a major metropolitan center has pushed development onto some of Florida’s most challenging geology. The underlying limestone of the Ocala Platform is prone to dissolution, creating a karst landscape where sandy surface soils can bridge hidden voids. A properly engineered raft or mat foundation distributes structural loads across a broad footprint, reducing the risk of differential settlement when those sandy lenses shift or consolidate unevenly. Our geotechnical team integrates site-specific standard penetration testing data with high-resolution ground-penetrating radar to map the rockhead before recommending a rigid mat or a post-tensioned raft slab. In a city that averages over 50 inches of rain annually, controlling moisture-related volume change in the near-surface soils is just as critical as managing the deeper dissolution features.

In Orlando’s karst terrain, a mat foundation is not just a slab—it is a structural bridge spanning potential subsurface voids.

Method and coverage

The design process starts with a track-mounted drill rig equipped with automatic SPT hammers confirming ASTM D1586 energy ratios, boring through the fine quartz sands typical of the Orlando area to refusal on the weathered limestone. Every mat foundation we specify emerges from a sequenced analysis: first establishing the allowable bearing pressure using presumptive values adjusted by in-situ test data, then modeling the soil-structure interaction in finite element software to map moment and shear envelopes for the mat’s thickness. In Orange County, where the water table often sits within three to five feet of the surface, buoyancy and uplift checks under flood conditions become a governing load case. We integrate the triaxial shear strength parameters from undisturbed samples to refine the modulus of subgrade reaction, ensuring the mat’s predicted settlement stays within tolerable limits for the superstructure.

Regional considerations

ASCE 7-22 and the Florida Building Code explicitly require geotechnical investigations to address sinkhole activity in karst regions, a mandate that hits home in Orlando where the limestone lies shallow and the historical sinkhole density ranks among the highest in the state. A conventional isolated footing can fail catastrophically if a small void migrates to the surface directly under one column, but a continuous mat bridges across the collapsed zone and redistributes the load to adjacent competent ground. The secondary risk in Orlando is long-term creep settlement in the loose, saturated sands that dominate the upper 20 to 30 feet; a mat foundation keeps total settlement uniform even if the absolute magnitude exceeds what a single footing could tolerate. Designing without a thorough karst assessment invites sudden differential movement that can shear utility connections and rack structural frames.

Top questions

Why is a mat foundation often preferred over isolated footings in Orlando?

In Orlando’s karst environment, the limestone bedrock can contain solution cavities and soft raveled zones that create unpredictable bearing conditions under individual footings. A mat foundation spreads the load across the entire footprint, so if one small area loses support due to a void migration, the slab bridges across it. The Florida Building Code recommends this redundancy for sinkhole-prone sites.

What is the typical cost range for designing a mat foundation for a residential project in Orlando?

For a single-family home or small commercial building in Orlando, the geotechnical investigation and structural design of a raft or mat foundation typically falls between US$1.200 and US$3.710 depending on lot size, number of borings, and whether post-tensioning is required.

How deep do you need to investigate the soil for a mat foundation here?

We usually extend borings to at least twice the mat’s shorter dimension in depth, or to competent rock if it is shallower. In Orlando that means 30 to 50 feet of exploration through the surficial sands and into the top of the Ocala Limestone, with continuous SPT sampling and rock quality designation recorded for the weathered caprock.
