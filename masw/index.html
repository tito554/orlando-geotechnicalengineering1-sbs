MASW Testing and VS30 Shear Wave Velocity Surveys in Orlando, Florida

Together, we solve the challenges of tomorrow.

DISCOVER →

A six-story medical office building near Lake Eola required a Site Class determination before the structural engineer could finalize the lateral load path. The geotechnical boring logs showed loose to medium dense sands down to 45 feet, but the IBC Section 1613.2 demands a measured Vs30 value, not an assumed one. We mobilized a 24-channel seismograph with 4.5 Hz geophones along the proposed building footprint and ran two orthogonal MASW lines. The survey took four hours on a Tuesday morning in August. The resulting shear wave velocity profile—Vs30 of 238 m/s—placed the site firmly in Site Class D, avoiding the costly detailing requirements of Site Class E that a nearby property triggered. When you need a defensible MASW / VS30 (shear wave velocity) measurement for an Orlando project, the method must account for the shallow limestone pinnacles and sinkhole-prone sands that define Central Florida geology. We often pair the survey with CPT soundings to verify the depth to competent bearing strata, especially east of I-4 where the Hawthorne Formation creates abrupt impedance contrasts.

A measured Vs30 profile replaces the default Site Class D assumption with a defensible number, often saving structural steel tonnage on mid-rise projects across Central Florida.

Method and coverage

The field setup for an Orlando MASW survey typically uses a 24-channel Geometrics Geode seismograph connected to a spread of 4.5 Hz vertical geophones spaced at 5-foot intervals. The source is an 8-pound sledgehammer striking a steel plate; the impact generates Rayleigh waves that propagate through the surficial sands and underlying limestone. Because Orlando sits on the Floridan aquifer system, near-surface saturation can attenuate high-frequency energy—our processing workflow applies a dispersion curve inversion with a layered earth model constrained by available boring data. We sample the upper 100 feet of the soil column, which is the depth range that controls Vs30 per ASCE 7-22. The data acquisition follows ASTM D4428M-14 guidelines for seismic crosshole testing, adapted for surface wave methods. Post-processing uses SurfSeis or WinMASW software to pick the fundamental mode and invert for the Vs profile; the team cross-checks results against SPT blow counts from any nearby borings to validate the shear wave velocity trend.
When the dispersion image shows a well-defined fundamental mode down to 30 Hz, uncertainty in the Vs30 calculation stays below 15%, which satisfies the peer review requirements of most Orlando structural engineering firms.

Regional considerations

Orlando's population surpassed 320,000 in 2024, and the construction pace along the I-4 corridor has exposed a recurring geotechnical hazard: sinkhole-induced differential settlement masked as seismic site amplification. The 2017 M5.2 earthquake near Daytona—though modest—reminded structural engineers that Florida is not aseismic; the USGS National Seismic Hazard Model assigns a 1–2% in 50-year PGA of 0.02–0.04g to Orange County. The greater risk in Orlando is the karst terrain. Solution cavities in the Ocala Limestone create abrupt lateral changes in shear wave velocity that a single boring cannot capture. A MASW / VS30 (shear wave velocity) survey images these velocity contrasts across a continuous transect; when the inverted Vs profile shows a velocity inversion below 20 feet—soft clay within a weathered limestone conduit—we flag it for grouting program evaluation before the foundation design proceeds. Overlooking these features has led to floor slab distress in at least two warehouse projects near the Florida Turnpike that we have been called to investigate.

Top questions

How much does a MASW / Vs30 survey cost for a typical Orlando commercial building lot?

For a standard single-building lot with two orthogonal MASW lines totaling approximately 300 linear feet, the survey cost ranges from US$1,620 to US$3,280. The price depends on site accessibility, surface conditions (asphalt versus grass), and whether existing boring logs are available to constrain the inversion model. Sites with heavy vegetation or active construction traffic fall at the higher end due to additional setup time.

How does Orlando's karst geology affect the MASW results?

The alternating sequence of sands, silts, and limestone pinnacles in the Floridan aquifer system creates high lateral variability in shear wave velocity. A limestone pinnacle with Vs above 800 m/s can sit adjacent to a raveled zone with Vs below 200 m/s. The MASW dispersion curve averages over the geophone spread length; we mitigate this by running multiple overlapping spreads and comparing the inverted Vs profiles against any available SPT data. When a velocity inversion appears—soft material beneath stiffer material—the team evaluates whether it represents a true karst feature or a processing artifact.

What is the difference between Site Class D and Site Class E in Orlando?

Site Class D corresponds to Vs30 between 180 and 360 m/s—most Orlando sites with medium-dense sands and shallow limestone fall here. Site Class E applies when Vs30 is below 180 m/s, typically found in areas with thick organic or loose alluvial deposits, or when more than 10 feet of soft clay exists. The structural impact is significant: Site Class E triggers higher seismic design category coefficients, which can increase lateral force demands by 30–50% compared to Site Class D. A measured Vs30 value can sometimes reclassify a site from E to D when the default assumption is overly conservative.

How long does it take to receive the final Vs30 report after the field survey?

The standard turnaround is five to seven business days. The processing involves dispersion curve picking, inversion with multiple starting models to test sensitivity, and integration with available boring logs. Expedited two-day delivery is available for projects on a tight permit deadline. The final report includes the processed Vs profile, Vs30 calculation, IBC Site Class letter, and a summary of the field acquisition parameters.
